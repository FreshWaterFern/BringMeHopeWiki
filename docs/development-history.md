# Development history

Bring Me Hope is a solo project with an unusual constraint: it is being built over a full
decade. Fern Nigro began it in **mid-2019**, originally under the name **Bring Me
Purpose**, and entered it into the [Decade Jam](https://itch.io/jam/decadejam), billed as
the world's first ten-year-long game jam, which runs from 2019 to 2029. A countdown to
that deadline still ticks on fernmakesgames.com. Everything in the game, the art, music,
writing, and code, is made by one person in GameMaker.

What follows is the story of how it has come together, phase by phase.

> **By the numbers (mid-2026):** about seven years in development, more than 2,000
> commits, one developer handling art, music, writing, and code, and a ten-year deadline
> in 2029.

## Origins (2019 to 2020)

The game started in mid-2019 as *Bring Me Purpose*, meant to channel years of accumulated
feeling into something playable. Fern has described the goal plainly: to make a game that
expresses "every single emotion that I have felt over the past 28 years." The decade-long
jam gave that ambition a frame, a finish line far enough away to build something genuinely
large.

The early going was not smooth. Fern began her transition in 2020, a significant and
demanding personal change, and development moved unevenly through that stretch before
finding firmer footing the following year.

## 2021: Foundation

By early 2021 the project moved into the repository it still lives in, and was renamed
along the way. This was the busiest year of raw construction: inventory, menus, the
interface, and resolution handling all took shape. Unglamorous plumbing, but it became the
skeleton everything later hung on.

## 2022: Art and identity

2022 is when the game started to look like itself. Fern built the Sea City tilesets,
redrew the protagonist Alium's animations, and designed the Frosted Caves with a hidden
boss. April was, in Fern's words, the most productive month of her life, coinciding with a
move toward living and working independently. A bout of COVID earlier in the year was a
real setback, but the recovery did not stop the momentum.

September brought the first marquee boss, Guppo, a cursed knight, alongside new bloom and
motion-blur shaders and a custom hitbox-debugging tool. The devlog from that month set the
tone for the years ahead: "Creative work on large projects is a marathon, not a sprint."
The commit history agrees; mid-2022 shows a clear slowdown as a day job competed for time.
Underneath it all was a belief Fern returns to often: "Converting emotions into an
experience is a skill that you have to practice regularly."

## 2023: Systems and story

2023 was about turning a pile of scenes into a game with structure. Early in the year Fern
overhauled the lighting, moving from additive to normal blending with tone mapping and
over-exposure, and set down visual-hierarchy rules so the world would read clearly at a
glance.

By spring the cutscene code had been rebuilt into a proper quest framework of conditions
and actions, and the story, until then mostly conceptual, was formalized in Obsidian. This
is also where scope became impossible to ignore; the May devlog was titled, with feeling,
"A Story About A Game That's Too Big All Of The Time."

The back half of the year delivered a major Frosted Caves expansion (a level-gated area,
four mini-bosses, and unique items), the elemental interaction system that still underpins
fire, ice, and oil, an experimental parry, and a redesigned main menu. A period of
personal change also led Fern to begin streaming daily, which sharpened focus and built a
small, supportive community around the work.

## 2024: Refinement

The standout change of 2024 was structural: the inventory was redesigned from a
twelve-slot backpack into the body-part equipment system the game uses today, so that
"every item feels special" instead of becoming "chaos pretty quickly." Nearly the entire
soundtrack was recomposed. Later came the Lush Caverns and a mini-boss, bridge physics,
rim lighting, softer overall lighting, and the Froglive NPC. A year of polish, captured by
the December devlog's title: "The Little Things."

## 2025: The most productive year

2025 was, by Fern's own account and the commit history alike, the most productive stretch
yet. Sea Cave City was finished as the first complete hub town, after roughly two years of
work, with three dungeons, a boss, around ten NPCs, and quests. Attention turned to Sand
City and Act 2, a fishing system arrived, and the opening got a new splash screen and
cutscenes. On the outreach side, Fern expanded to YouTube. The devlog title said it best:
"Every year I fall more in love with this game, and I like to think that it shows."

## An intermission: Gum Squad (late 2025 to early 2026)

For about three months around the new year, Bring Me Hope went quiet while Fern built a
different game entirely: Gum Squad, a multiplayer shooter, on a deliberate three-month
deadline. It was a conscious experiment in finishing: "I give myself a year, two years,
why not ten years? But this time I wanted to change the rules." The discipline of a hard
deadline was the point, and good practice for the marathon waiting at home.

## 2026: Return and acceleration

Bring Me Hope came back with force in the spring of 2026, when April became the single most
active month in the project's history. The combat gained grappling and throwing, the
Crystal Subway received a visual overhaul, and a long run of new items and per-item
tracking filled out the loot. The game remains in active early-access development, still
aimed at a 20 to 40 hour adventure, and still on its decade-long clock.

## The story so far

Across roughly seven years, a few themes have stayed constant: a tug-of-war between
ambition and the need to finish, a conviction that craft (light, sound, animation, feel)
is where emotion lives, and a willingness to build in the open. The Decade Jam ends in
2029. Bring Me Hope is well on its way there.
